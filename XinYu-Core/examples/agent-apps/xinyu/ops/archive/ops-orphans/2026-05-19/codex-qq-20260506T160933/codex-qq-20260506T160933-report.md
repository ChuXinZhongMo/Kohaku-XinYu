## Paper Information

### Paper 1: Google / Computational Life

- Full title: **Computational Life: How Well-formed, Self-replicating Programs Emerge from Simple Interaction**
- Authors: Blaise Agüera y Arcas, Jyrki Alakuijala, James Evans, Ben Laurie, Alexander Mordvintsev, Eyvind Niklasson, Ettore Randazzo, Luca Versari.
- Affiliation/source: Google Paradigms of Intelligence Team; James Evans is affiliated with The University of Chicago.
- Venue/status: arXiv:2406.19108, cs.NE/cs.AI. Submitted 2024-06-27; v2 revised 2024-08-02. Google Research publication page lists it as arXiv 2024.
- Links:
  - https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.19108
  - https://research.google/pubs/computational-life-how-well-formed-self-replicating-programs-emerge-from-simple-interaction/

Core mechanism:

- Substrate: extensions of Brainfuck, mainly BFF, plus related Forth and machine-instruction substrates.
- Self-modification: program code and data share the same tape. Programs execute read/write instructions that can overwrite themselves and other programs.
- Interaction model: a large fixed population, usually `2^17` programs, each 64 bytes. At each epoch, random ordered pairs are concatenated, executed for a fixed budget or until termination, then split back into two programs.
- Explicit target generation: none.
- Fitness/selection: no explicit fitness function. Selection is implicit: strings that can copy themselves into other strings occupy more of the soup. The paper frames this as competition for scarce resources such as space, execution time, and sometimes energy.
- Experimental measurements: state transitions are tracked via high-order entropy, compression-based complexity, unique token tracing, and takeover dynamics. The authors test runs with and without background mutations and conclude the transitions mainly arise from self-modification plus interaction, not from pure random initialization or random mutation.

### Paper 2: UCL / ASAL++

- Full title: **Guiding Evolution of Artificial Life Using Vision-Language Models**
- Authors: Nikhil Baid, Hannah Erlebach, Paul Hellegouarch, Frederico Wieser.
- Affiliation/source: University College London, UK; Paul Hellegouarch also lists Institut Pasteur / Université Paris Cité / CNRS UMR 3571.
- Venue/status: arXiv:2509.22447, submitted 2025-09-26. The arXiv page states it was accepted for the Proceedings of the Artificial Life Conference 2025, MIT Press.
- Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.22447

Core mechanism:

- Substrate: Lenia, rendered as 2D video rollouts.
- Inner optimization: ASAL searches simulation parameters `theta` so rendered rollouts semantically match natural-language target prompts in a VLM embedding space. The paper describes CLIP-style image/text embeddings and Sep-CMA-ES/CMA-ES optimization.
- Evolutionary target generation: ASAL++ adds a second foundation model, Gemma-3-4b-it, as an EVOLVER MODEL. It receives the previous rollout video and prompt context, then proposes the next target prompt.
- Two strategies:
  - EST, Evolved Supervised Targets: optimize only toward the newest generated prompt at each iteration.
  - ETT, Evolved Temporal Targets: optimize toward the whole prompt history so far.
- Fitness/selection: explicit optimization loss, roughly negative similarity between rollout embeddings and prompt embeddings. CMA-ES asks for candidate parameters, evaluates rollout loss, and updates the population distribution with `tell`.
- Experimental design: 9 human-chosen initial prompts, 8 ASAL++ iterations, 2000 ASAL optimization steps per iteration, Lenia rollouts of 256 frames. The paper also reports OE score as a novelty measure and explores prompt-tree variants.

## Direct Correspondence Map

- `ObjectiveScheduler.choose()` in the XinYu code corresponds only at the design-pattern level to ASAL++: both use an outer loop that changes targets over time. Difference: ASAL++ uses Gemma-3 and visual history; XinYu uses hand-coded metric thresholds and weights.
- `Genome.self_patch()` corresponds only at the conceptual level to the Google paper's self-modification theme. Difference: Google self-modification is code/data tape rewriting during program execution; XinYu self-patch is supervised weight-table correction after prediction feedback.
- `_next_population()` corresponds more to a classic genetic algorithm than to either paper. ASAL++ uses CMA-ES over simulation parameters; Google uses no explicit selection function.
- `Genome.predict_self()` and the `self_awareness_index` formula do not have a specific source in either paper. They are custom operational-proxy machinery added by the previous Codex implementation.
- The design document itself names the target scheduler as ASAL++ inspired, but does not claim a direct implementation of ASAL++.

## Conclusion

The prior Codex self-awareness agent experiment is **not the algorithm of either paper**.

It is a composite design:

1. From the Google paper it borrows the broad idea that bounded self-modification inside a simple artificial-life substrate can produce interesting emergent structure. It does not reproduce BFF, primordial soup interaction, implicit takeover selection, high-order entropy analysis, or code-tape self-replication.
2. From ASAL++ it borrows the outer-loop idea of changing evolutionary targets over time. It does not use Gemma-3, CLIP/VLM embeddings, Lenia, EST/ETT prompt histories, or CMA-ES.
3. The most important part for the reported `self_awareness_index` 0.813, namely explicit self-state prediction plus metric-weighted selection, appears to be a custom Codex-built operational proxy. It is not directly sourced from either of the two papers.

Best classification: **ASAL++-inspired target scheduling + Google-inspired self-modification motif + custom self-model/GA experiment**.
